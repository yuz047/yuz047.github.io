Market structure series

Prediction markets are becoming market structure.

Polymarket, Kalshi, and brokerage event contracts turn real-world events into tradable risk. The price looks like a probability, but the market underneath is shaped by liquidity, regulation, bots, insiders, and settlement rules.

Thesis: prediction-market odds are useful market prices, not clean truth signals. A 63-cent YES contract can contain beliefs, fees, stale quotes, market-maker inventory, bot flow, contract wording, and settlement risk at the same time.

Prediction Markets 101

A prediction market asks a contract-level question: will an event happen by the stated settlement rule? A YES contract often pays $1 if the event occurs and zero if it does not. When YES trades at $0.63, the common shortcut is to call it a 63% probability.

That shortcut is useful, but incomplete. The price is a market price. It can reflect collective belief, but it can also reflect liquidity, fees, funding costs, market-maker quotes, bot activity, position limits, wording ambiguity, and dispute risk.

The CFTC describes event contracts as yes/no instruments with fixed payouts and notes that prices may reflect perceived likelihood. The important word is may: perceived probability travels through market structure before it becomes a traded price.

Venue wrapper matters

Prediction markets are no longer one product category. Polymarket represents a crypto-native, internet-first path. Kalshi represents a regulated event-exchange path. Robinhood represents mainstream retail distribution. The payoff can look similar while the oversight, user base, execution environment, and surveillance obligations differ materially.

Crypto-native venue

Wallet flow, fast market creation, global attention, and jurisdictional complexity.

Regulated exchange

Contract-market rules, listing review, surveillance expectations, and federal jurisdiction questions.

Brokerage distribution

Retail-product framing, customer disclosures, suitability-adjacent controls, and support escalation.

Research use

Market-implied odds can be an input, but should be treated like noisy order-book data rather than final truth.

When event contracts become brokerage market structure

The regulatory question is not simply “derivative or gambling?” A more useful risk lens is contract-level classification: whether the event has a hedging use, whether the outcome is manipulable, whether an insider population exists, and whether the resolution source is objective and auditable.

Retail brokerage distribution changes the scale of the problem. A product that once lived on specialist venues can become a mainstream active-trader product. That creates familiar market-structure questions: volume quality, customer understanding, concentration, surveillance, and post-event dispute handling.

A better screening framework

Market operators and brokerages should classify contracts by event type, manipulability, insider population, resolution clarity, venue surveillance coverage, and customer disclosure burden. A Fed decision contract, a weather contract, a sports contract, and an election contract may share yes/no payoff mechanics, but they should not automatically receive the same risk treatment.

Bots, manipulation, and mispriced contracts

Prediction markets look clean on the surface: a question, two sides, and a price between zero and one dollar. Underneath, they are microstructure systems. A displayed price may reflect crowd belief; it may also reflect stale quotes, thin books, latency, insider information, wash-like volume, ambiguous wording, or a fragile resolution source.

Helpful automation

Market-making bots can tighten spreads. Arbitrage bots can align related contracts. News-driven systems can update stale markets after public information appears.

Adverse automation

Fast systems can snipe stale quotes, amplify misleading volume, route around weak controls, or exploit settlement wording faster than casual users can react.

Manipulation has a wider attack surface

In equities, manipulation usually targets price. In prediction markets, manipulation can target price, the event, the resolution source, or the timing of public information. If settlement depends on a data feed, the data feed becomes part of the market. If settlement depends on a public announcement, the announcement process becomes part of the market.

Brokerage risk view

Prediction markets should be evaluated like market structure, not like novelty content. A credible control stack needs contract approval, insider-risk mapping, market surveillance, bot/API governance, resolution governance, customer disclosures, and post-settlement review.
